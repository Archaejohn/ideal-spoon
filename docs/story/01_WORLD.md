## 1. The Setting — Aetheris

Aetheris is a sky. Where other worlds have ground underfoot, Aetheris has **skylands** — islands of stone and soil that float in a bright, endless air, lit by a sun that never quite sets the same way twice. Far below lies the **Underland**, the world's old surface, now drowned in a slow ocean of silver mist. People say the mist eats sound, then light, then memory. Almost nobody goes down. Almost nobody comes back up.

Long ago there were thousands of skylands, close enough to throw a rope between. Now there are dozens that still ride high, and the rest are sinking, one quiet inch at a time, into the mist. The remaining islands drift slowly on the high winds, and their positions are charted like tides. **Skyships** — wooden hulls slung under canvas wings and lifted by a glowing **wellstone** keel — hop between them along mapped wind-roads called the **Sky-Roads**. A short hop between neighbor islands takes an afternoon; crossing from one end of the known sky to the other takes weeks, and crews swap charts like gold.

### The Skylands the party will visit

1. **Hollowgate** — *the hub skytown.* A patchwork city built across three linked islands, lashed together with bridge-chains and crane-arms, around the ribs of a beached Skyborn skyship the size of a cathedral. Markets, dock-cranes, the Lantern Order's great lamp, a salvagers' guild, and every kind of sky-folk. Smells of tar, hot bread, and ozone. *Danger:* none in the streets — but the deep undermarket has thieves, and the old wreck below the city hums with things that should be asleep.

2. **Meadowmoor** — *Wren's home.* A small, green farming skyland of windmills, sheep-on-tethers, and one failing lamp at its heart. Gentle, sleepy, beloved. *Danger:* its wellstone is nearly spent, so faint **Hollow** wisps drift in from the dimming edges — the first creatures players meet.

3. **The Glasswastes** — *a shattered skyland of fused white sand and mirror-glass*, where a Skyborn workshop once burned too bright. Heat-shimmer, ringing silence, half-buried machines. *Danger:* **Glass Hollow** — husks sheathed in mirror-shards that scatter light and confuse the eye.

4. **Verdance** — *a wild jungle-island gone enormous*, where untended aether made the plants grow strange and huge. Floating pollen, singing flowers, vine-bridges. *Danger:* overgrown garden-constructs that mistake travelers for weeds to be tidied.

5. **The Cinderworks** — *the Ascendancy's grim industrial skyland*, all furnaces, harvester-docks, and aether-pumps draining smaller islands dry. Soot-clouds, marching order, foghorns. *Danger:* Ascendancy patrols and **Tinplate** automatons — and the moral danger of seeing good people serving a wrong cause.

6. **Thornholt** — *a free salvagers' shantytown bolted onto a tilted island*, half sunk and proud of it. Ramps, junk-art, loud music, the best black-market relics in the sky. *Danger:* rival salvage crews, rickety footing, and curiosity that wanders into wrecks that bite.

7. **Stillmere** — *a quiet monastery-island of the Lantern Order*, terraced gardens around a mirror-still reservoir of pure aether-light. Bells, candle-boats, hush. *Danger:* not monsters but secrets — the Order keeps something here it has not told the world.

8. **The Hush** — *a skyland already half-claimed by the Fading*, dim and grey, where aether has nearly gone and the Hollow are bold. Visited late, after the mid-game cataclysm. *Danger:* dense Hollow, sinking ground, and the lesson of what the whole sky becomes if no one acts.

*(Beneath them all, reached only in Act IV, lies the descent toward the **Wellspring** itself — down through the mist, where the rules of the sky stop being friendly.)*

## 2. Aether & the Song

**Aether** is the light-that-does-work. To ordinary people it is simply magic: it lifts ships, warms lamps, runs clever tools. In truth it is the leftover energy of the vanished **Skyborn**, who learned to draw a kind of living current up from the world's heart and store it. It looks like slow, warm light — gold at full strength, fading to thin blue, then grey as it dies.

**Wellstones** are the batteries of the world: smooth crystals, grown not mined, that hold charged aether. A house has a lamp-stone; a ship has a keel-stone; a city has a great-stone. Wellstones can be charged at a **wellspring-tap** (rare) or topped from another stone (common, lossy). When a stone runs grey, whatever it powered simply stops — the lamp goes out, the ship sinks toward the mist.

**Resonants** are the rare few who can *hear* aether directly — a music under the world that they call **the Song**. A Resonant can do three felt things, and these are the rules players can count on:

- **Listen** — sense aether nearby: how much, how healthy, and whether Hollow are near. (Always available; the party's "radar.")
- **Steady** — calm and stretch a wellstone's charge, or briefly relight a dim one, by humming it back into tune. (Cheap, but small.)
- **Shape** — actively move and weave aether: mend, ward, push, wake a sleeping construct. (Powerful, but costly.)

**The limits and costs are firm and consistent:**
- A Resonant cannot *create* aether, only *move, tune, and shape* what already exists. No free energy. Ever.
- Shaping draws on the Resonant's own steadiness. Overreach brings **Songsickness** — ringing ears, grey vision, exhaustion. Push too far and a Resonant can go **Hollow-touched** for a time, losing their Song until they rest and heal. (This is the cliff Marrow fell off of.)
- The Song works *with* aether, so it grows weaker wherever aether is fading. In dead places a Resonant is nearly deaf and nearly powerless — which is exactly where the danger is worst.

**Hollow** creatures are aether's absence given shape. When a place is drained below a threshold, the husks of whatever was there — animals, machines, even old constructs — stir as **Hollow**: hollowed-out, resonance-empty things that move toward the last warmth of aether to fill the gap they feel. They are not demons and not cruel; they are *hungry quiet*. They drift toward lit lamps, charged stones, and Resonants. They can be driven off, out-waited, or — by a Resonant — *re-tuned* and calmed, which is the kindest and hardest answer. The more the world fades, the more Hollow there are. This is the ticking clock of the whole story.

**Why aether is fading:** the Wellspring that feeds the whole sky has been running down, untended, since the Skyborn vanished. Every wellstone in the world is, ultimately, a leaf drawing from one slowly-dimming root. Harvesting harder (the Ascendancy's plan) does not make more — it just spends the last of it faster, in fewer hands.

## 3. The Skyborn & the Wellspring

The **Skyborn** were the makers: a people (or perhaps more than one people) who, ages ago, reached down into the world's heart and woke the **Wellspring**, a vast machine-that-breathes-light buried below the mist. With its current they lifted the lands into the sky and built a civilization of impossible grace — singing towers, ships like cathedrals, constructs that thought and helped. Their relics still litter the skylands, half-understood and prized.

Then came the **Sundering** — a catastrophe the Skyborn neither fully explain nor survive in the record. In a single span the sky cracked, many lands fell, and the Skyborn were *gone*: not all dead in any tomb anyone has found, simply **absent**, as if they stepped out of the world mid-sentence. What they left behind: the floating lands, the fading current, scattered constructs, and a sealed gate down to the Wellspring.

Inside the Wellspring sleeps the **Warden** (the Skyborn called it the **Heartmind**) — a dormant intelligence built to tend the current and, some say, to keep a promise the makers made. It is not awake, not evil, and not simply a machine. The central mystery threads three unanswered questions, and the world bible **plants them without answering** (the endings resolve them):

- *Did the Skyborn die in the Sundering — or did they leave on purpose, and go somewhere?*
- *Is the Warden a guardian holding the world together, a lock keeping something out, or a sleeper waiting to be asked the right question?*
- *Was the Sundering an accident, a sacrifice, or a choice someone made — and is the world repeating it now?*

The construct **Bramble**, whom Wren awakens, carries broken fragments of Skyborn memory — enough to point toward these questions, never enough to settle them until the player does.

## 4. Factions

### The Ascendancy
A mighty, orderly sky-nation centered on the **Cinderworks** and its lifted citadel-cities. Facing the undeniable math of the Fading, the Ascendancy has chosen **triage**: drain the Wellspring's remaining current to lift *their* cities higher and longer, outlasting the fall — saving some by spending everyone else's share. Their methods are harvester-fleets, conscription of Resonants, and relentless, joyless efficiency. They are *sincere*: their leaders genuinely believe scattering the last aether thinly saves no one, while hoarding it saves a remnant. **High Regent Corvus Thane** grieves the world even as he dooms most of it; his enforcer **Marrow**, a fallen Resonant, is the living warning of despair. The Ascendancy view the decline clearly — and conclude that mercy means choosing who survives.

### The Lantern Order
A gentle, ancient faith that venerates the Skyborn as benevolent makers and tends the wellstone **lamps** of the world as holy duty. Their creed: *keep the world lit, keep faith, and the makers will provide.* Healers like **Mira** do real good; their bells and candle-boats give people hope. But the Order meets the evidence of the Fading with denial — and, at **Stillmere**, it guards a secret it fears will shatter that faith if revealed. Their collision with the truth (faith vs. evidence) is a quiet, painful one, and the brief's branch lets players expose or protect the Order's secret.

### Free Sky-folk, Traders & Salvagers
The independent middle of the world: town councils, trade convoys, smugglers, and the **salvagers' guilds** of **Thornholt** and **Hollowgate** who pull Skyborn relics from wrecks. **Captain Sable Vance** is one of these — pragmatic, wry, homeless since her own island sank. The sky-folk distrust both the Ascendancy's grand plan and the Order's prayers; they just want to keep flying. They view aether's decline as a problem to *out-fly and out-trade* — until the story shows them you cannot.

### The Keepers (invented)
A loose, dwindling fellowship of **aether-tenders** — small-island lamp-keepers like Wren's master, who learned by hand to nurse failing wellstones and steady the Song without dogma or empire. Not a power, barely a guild; more a tradition. They are the quiet conscience that says the world is a *garden*, not a *mine*. Wren is the last apprentice of this nearly-vanished craft.

### The Skyborn & the Warden
The absent makers and the sleeping **Heartmind** are less a faction than the gravity every faction orbits. Each group has decided what the Skyborn *would have wanted* — and built their cause on that guess.

## 5. History & Timeline

Dates are counted in years **After the Sundering (A.S.)**. The deep past is reckoned in the **Skyborn Age (S.A.)**, mostly legend.

- **The Skyborn Age (S.A., ages ago).** The makers wake the Wellspring, lift the lands, and build the singing civilization. The sky is crowded with islands and light.
- **Year 0 — The Sundering.** The sky cracks; many lands fall into the new mist; the Skyborn vanish in a single span. The Wellspring's gate seals. The current begins, slowly, to dim.
- **The Scattering (0–200 A.S.).** Survivors regroup on the lands that remain aloft. The art of charging wellstones is half-relearned, half-lost. The Lantern Order is founded to "keep the world lit."
- **The Long Lull (200–900 A.S.).** A deceptively stable age. Aether fades so slowly that generations barely notice. Sky-trade flourishes; Hollowgate grows from a wreck into a city.
- **The Quickening Fade (900–990 A.S.).** The decline becomes undeniable: lamps gutter, edge-islands sink within a single lifetime, Hollow grow common. The Ascendancy rises on the promise of a hard answer.
- **Present day (≈990 A.S.).** Meadowmoor's lamp is dying. Ascendancy harvesters reach the small islands. **Wren's Song awakens.** *(The story begins.)*
- **The Second Sundering (mid-story).** The Ascendancy forces a partial Wellspring drain; the **Fading** lurches forward, reshaping the map and scattering the party. *(The Act II cataclysm.)*

## 6. Daily Life & Culture

For most people, life on a skyland is small, sturdy, and lit by a single beloved lamp. Folk wake to the **lamp-bell**, work the terraced fields and rooftop gardens, and trade by skyship for what their island cannot grow.

- **Food & trade.** Skylands grow what their soil and aether allow: windward wheat, tether-sheep, cloud-melons, root vegetables, and **lamp-herbs** that glow faintly when fresh. Salt, metal, and fresh wellstone-charge are the great imports; nearly everything arrives by ship, so dock-day is the week's heartbeat. A common treat is **sun-bread**, baked on hot lamp-stones until it crackles.
- **Belief.** Most people half-follow the Lantern Order: they hang a small lamp by the door, "tune in" at the festival bells, and tell children the Skyborn will return when the world is kept bright enough. Sailors keep private superstitions about the mist below. Resonants are regarded with a mix of awe and unease — wonderful, and a little uncanny.
- **Fears.** The two great fears are **the sinking** (your island slipping into the mist) and **the Hollow** (the quiet things that come when the lamp goes grey). Parents calm children with the same words: *"The lamp is lit. We're aloft. All's well."*
- **Festivals.** The **Relighting** marks the day each island's great-stone was last recharged — lanterns are floated on the wind, songs are sung to "tune the year bright," sun-bread is baked, and folk dance on the bridges late into the night. *(In play, a Relighting the party makes possible — by steadying a town's failing lamp — is a **marquee joy scene**: thousands of floating lanterns, music, the whole found family laughing together, and the Song heard **soaring**, whole and golden. Light is allowed to be only glad.)* **Mistfall Eve** is a cozy, slightly spooky night of stories about the Underland, ending with everyone lighting their smallest lamp together.
- **Wonder (for a young reader).** Imagine ships with glowing keels skating along invisible wind-roads; flowers on Verdance that hum when you walk past; a tiny clockwork friend who remembers a vanished world; whales of mist seen far below; and the moment a frightened kid realizes the music only *she* can hear might be the thing that saves everyone. The world is bright, strange, and kind at its edges — danger is real, but so is rescue, friendship, and the next island over the horizon.

When loss comes, it is shown gently: the dead "go into the **Long Quiet**," fading to a soft light rather than dying gorily; a sunk island is mourned like a lost friend; even the Hollow are pitied as much as feared.

## 8. Visual & Musical Hooks

**Color & light.** Aether is the world's palette: healthy gold, anxious thin-blue, dead grey. Lamps and keels glow warm against cool sky. The Fading reads on screen as the slow draining of color toward grey at an island's edges. Hollow are *absences* — silhouettes rimmed in faint cold light, ash-and-static, never bloody.

**Iconic images.** A small girl with a hand on a guttering lamp, listening. Skyships skating along glowing wind-roads between drifting islands. The beached Skyborn cathedral-ship that is now Hollowgate. Candle-boats floated on a still mirror-reservoir at Stillmere. Mirror-glass deserts that ring like a struck bell. Giant humming flowers on Verdance. A tiny clockwork companion whose chest-stone flickers like a heartbeat.

**Motion & scale.** Everything drifts gently — clouds, islands, lantern-light — so that the *sinking* of a land reads as wrong and sad. Skyships feel like sailing, not flying: canvas, ropes, and wind. The descent to the Wellspring should feel like the world holding its breath.

**Music.** A central, simple melodic motif — **"the Song"** — that the player learns to recognize: heard whole and golden in safe, lit places; thin and faltering near the Fading; played in fragments by Bramble; and built to full, hopeful resolution at the best ending. Instruments: warm strings, glass-harmonica shimmer for aether, low mist-drones below, and a single child-clear voice or chime for Wren's listening. Hollow zones go quiet and tuneless — the music *leaving* is the scare, not a sting.

**Victory & celebration (the highs).** Joy gets its own sound. Wins land on a **victory fanfare** — a bright flourish that takes the Song's first three notes and blooms them into brass, chimes, and a full, *soaring* statement of the motif. Where the Fading is the Song thinning toward silence, triumph is the Song **soaring**: the same melody the player knows frightened and frayed, now lifted high, fast, and golden. Use it on the hard-won highs — the getaway and first flight, a town relit, a Hollow swarm calmed to cheers, the Relighting festival, and the dawn of the best ending — so the celebrations ring out as unmistakably as the losses ring hollow. The motif soaring at the top is the emotional rhyme that pays off every time it faltered below.

**Mood north star.** *Hopeful, wondrous, a little melancholy — the feeling of keeping one small light lit while the dark gets longer, and slowly learning the light is worth sharing.* Crucially, the melancholy only works against real **highs**: exhilarating wins, warm laughter, and out-loud celebration. Aim for emotional **range** — let the player whoop on the getaway and dance at the Relighting, so the Sundering and the goodbyes cut all the way down. Joy is not a betrayal of the heart; it is what makes the heart cost something.
