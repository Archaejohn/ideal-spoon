# Character Bible — "Aetherbound" (working title)

> Built on `00_BRIEF.md` (the anchor + Canon rulings) and `01_WORLD.md`. Every character here grows from the brief's cast skeleton and lives inside the world bible's skylands, factions, and rules. Battle roles assume a classic ATB (active-time-bar) system. Abilities are written as flavor, not numbers, so designers can balance them later.

---

## How to read this document

Each playable character has a **Snapshot**, **Look**, **Voice**, **Backstory**, **Arc** (Acts I–IV), **Gameplay identity**, and **Bonds**. Antagonists get **Snapshot**, **Look**, **Voice**, **Motivation**, **Method**, and **Confrontation**. A **Party dynamics** section and **Casting notes for art & music** close it out.

## Wren — the Heart

**Snapshot.** Wren · age 14 · apprentice aether-tender of Meadowmoor, secret Resonant · *The smallest person in the sky, holding the largest decision in her two hands.*

**Look.** Slight and a little hunched, like someone used to being overlooked. Patched canvas coat two sizes too big (her master's), the deep pockets stuffed with wicks, tuning-cloths, and a cracked lamp-glass. Hair the soft brown of windward wheat, always escaping its tie. A single signature detail: a small lamp-stone on a leather cord at her throat that glows brighter when she is afraid — the art team should treat it as her mood-light. Palette: warm wheat-gold and lamp-amber against the cool blues of the open sky. Silhouette reads as a child cupping a flame.

**Voice.** Earnest, quiet, and a *listener* — she answers questions a beat late because she was really hearing you. She asks more than she declares.
- "I don't know if I'm brave. But the lamp's still lit, and I'm still here, so — let's keep going."
- "Wait. Stop talking for a second. Can't you hear that? Something underneath everything... it's *hurting.*"
- "You don't fix a garden by burning the rest of it. My master taught me that. I think the whole world forgot."

**Backstory.** Wren was raised on Meadowmoor by old Keeper Pell, the island's lamp-keeper, after her parents went into the Long Quiet when she was small. She grew up nursing a failing wellstone by hand — and slowly realized she could *hear* something no one else could: a music under the lamp, the Song. She hid it. Resonants are looked at sideways, and Wren wanted, more than anything, to simply be a normal keeper of one small light. **Her wound:** she believes she is too little to matter. **Her want:** to keep her home safe and unchanged — a want the story will have to take from her so she can find a bigger one.

**Arc.**
- **Act I — The Keeper's Lamp.** Timid and hiding. When Ascendancy harvesters drain Meadowmoor and its lamp gutters out, Wren's Song awakens in front of everyone. She flees on Sable's ship not as a hero but as a frightened kid who just lost her whole world.
- **Act II — The Sinking Roads.** Wren learns the Song is the rarest gift in the sky — and that it makes her a target. She starts using it to help: steadying dying lamps on island after island. She meets Marrow and sees, with horror, a Resonant hollowed out by despair — *what she could become.*
- **Act III — The Fading World.** After the Second Sundering scatters the party, Wren survives the grey, broken map mostly alone for a stretch, nearly losing her Song in a dead zone. She comes out the other side having chosen hope on purpose, not by luck. She finds Keeper Pell again at The Hush — and loses him there, gently, as he keeps the last lamp lit by hand for the evacuation (A3-02b/c): her first real, permanent grief, and the personal wound the finale pays off. She becomes the person who reunites the family.
- **Act IV — The Wellspring.** Wren is the one who can speak with the Warden. The final choice runs through her — but the game has spent four acts proving she does not decide alone. She has learned that saving the world is not one magic fix; it is changing how everyone shares the light.

**Gameplay identity — Resonant Support / Utility ("Song" abilities).** Wren is the party's keystone: a flexible support who buffs, shields, calms enemies, and reveals the field. Low raw damage, enormous swing on the rest of the team. Her unique resource is **Breath** (her steadiness) — spend it to Shape, regain it by Listening between fights. Overspending risks a temporary **Songsickness** state (mirroring the world rule), making her power feel precious.
- **Listen** — reveals enemy weaknesses, hidden aether, and incoming Hollow; party gains a turn-order edge. (Her "radar," always available.)
- **Steady** — heals a little and clears one ailment by humming an ally "back into tune"; can relight a downed ally at low cost.
- **Kindling Chorus** — a growing party-wide shield that strengthens each turn it goes untouched (rewards planning).
- **Quiet the Hollow** — non-damaging crowd control: calms/pacifies a Hollow enemy instead of destroying it (the kindest, hardest option — and sometimes the only one that works).

**Bonds.** *Keeper Pell:* the master who raised her and taught her the keeper's craft — his habit (two taps, a breath, "the lamp is lit, we're aloft, all's well") and the Keeper's Lamp he pressed into her hands. He returns in Act III and is lost keeping a lamp lit; he is the recurring father-thread and the light she carries into the final choice. *Sable:* the wry aunt-captain who becomes the closest thing to family; Sable teaches Wren to be brave out loud (and the two fracture and reconcile in Act III over whether you can save everyone). *Tam:* her oldest friend and anchor — the one person who knew her before the Song. *Bramble:* a soul-deep kinship; both are caretakers of a fading world, and Wren is the only one who truly hears Bramble's broken music — which makes Bramble's Act IV gift the hardest loss of all. *Marrow:* her dark mirror, and — quietly — the relationship that scares and defines her most.

## Sable Vance — the Captain

**Snapshot.** Captain Sable Vance · age 38 · skyship captain & salvager, master of the *Driftwing* · *A homeless pilot with a sharp tongue and a softer heart than she'll admit.*

**Look.** Tall, weather-cured, built like someone who hauls her own rigging. A long salvager's coat sewn from a dozen mismatched sailcloths, each a different faded color — a wearable map of every island she's worked. Close-cropped grey-streaked black hair under a battered keel-pilot's cap. Signature detail: a brass spyglass worn at the hip like a sidearm, and a perpetual half-smirk. Palette: storm-grey, brass, and the warm tan of sun-worn leather. Silhouette: coat-tails snapping in the wind, hands in pockets, leaning like the deck's always tilting.

**Voice.** Dry, fast, deflecting. She makes jokes when feelings get close. Under the wit is real tenderness she'd deny under torture.
- "Oh, wonderful. A magic kid, a broken robot, and a missing god. Climb aboard, we're clearly going to live forever."
- "Rule one on my ship: nobody dies on my watch. Rule two: when I'm wrong about rule one, we make 'em pay for it."
- "Home's not a place, kid. I learned that the hard way, watching mine sink. Home's the people you keep aloft."

**Backstory.** Sable grew up on a mid-sky island called Larkfall that sank into the mist when she was nineteen — slowly, over a year, while the world shrugged. She got everyone she could onto ships and has been homeless and flying ever since, salvaging Skyborn relics for coin and telling herself she doesn't care about anyplace enough to lose it again. **Her wound:** survivor's grief, scarred over with cynicism. **Her want:** to never be powerless to save a home again — which she pretends is "to never have a home at all."

**Arc.** Cynic → believer. **Act I:** she takes Wren aboard for the salvage value of a Resonant, or so she claims. **Act II:** she keeps not leaving, keeps coming back, and starts to believe the world might be worth fighting for, not just out-flying. **Act III:** the Fading takes more islands and her old fatalism nearly wins — and it boils over into a real rift with Wren (A3-03b: "I lost my home twice; hope is a tax I can't pay" against Wren's insistence on saving everyone). A rescue-run she should not survive, and survives, reconciles them (A3-03c) and pulls her all the way over into hope. **Act IV:** she's the one who says out loud that they're not running anymore; they're going *down*, to the Wellspring, to fix the root. Early mentor who becomes a true believer.

**Gameplay identity — Gunner / Skyship Tricks.** High single-target burst and battlefield control via her sidearm and pilot's reflexes; off the field she's the party's pilot and the *Driftwing* is effectively a party member.
- **Snapshot** — a quick aimed shot that hits harder against revealed/weakened foes (combos off Wren's Listen).
- **Grapple-Line** — yanks a back-row enemy to the front, or pulls an ally out of danger (positioning control).
- **Warning Shot** — lowers an enemy's aggression/turn-speed; flavor: she'd rather scare a thing off than kill it.
- **Full Burn** — calls a strafing pass from the *Driftwing*: a big delayed hit that rewards setup.

**Bonds.** *Wren:* the daughter-she-didn't-plan; their banter (gruff captain, earnest kid) is the emotional spine of the early game — and their Act III fracture-and-reconcile (hoarding vs. sharing, between two who love each other) is the theme in miniature. *Tam:* she pretends his gadgets are junk and secretly funds them. *Kestrel:* prickly mutual respect — two disciplined survivors from opposite sides who learn to trust. *Rookwise:* old acquaintances with history; she's salvaged his "forbidden" relics before.

## Tam Brightgear — the Spark

**Snapshot.** Tam Brightgear · age 14 · self-taught inventor, Wren's best friend · *A bottomless well of bad-then-brilliant ideas.*

**Look.** Wiry and always in motion, smudged with grease and a little singed at the cuffs. A many-pocketed tool-vest bristling with half-built gadgets, goggles shoved up into a wild cloud of dark curls. One sleeve rolled higher than the other; mismatched boots. Signature detail: a clockwork "third hand" gauntlet he built himself, fingers made of relic-brass, that he gestures with when excited (which is always). Palette: copper, brass, and bright tinker's-paint primary colors against the soot. Silhouette: a kid mid-gesture, tools sticking out at every angle.

**Voice.** Excitable, rapid, tumbling over himself. Talks in exclamation points and course-corrections.
- "Okay okay okay — so it exploded, BUT, and hear me out, it exploded in a really *useful* direction!"
- "That's not junk, that's a pre-Sundering resonance coupler, do you have any idea — okay you don't, that's fine, I'm taking it."
- "I can fix it. I can totally fix it. ...I can probably fix it. Give me a wrench and ninety seconds."

**Backstory.** Tam grew up next door to Wren on Meadowmoor, the kid who took apart every broken tool on the island to see how it breathed. He learned aether-tech by trial and (mostly) error from salvaged Skyborn relics no one else could read. He follows Wren off the island without a second thought — she's his person. **His wound:** he's reckless, and one of his "fixes" has hurt someone he loved before (a younger sibling, lightly hurt, deeply remembered). **His want:** to prove cleverness can save people, not just impress them.

**Arc.** Reckless tinkerer → responsible maker. **Act I–II:** comic relief and gadget-engine, leaping before looking. **Act II climax:** a rushed invention of his fails at the worst moment during the partial drain, and he has to sit with consequences. **Act III:** he matures into the team's quartermaster, the one who *thinks two steps ahead* now, building the tools that actually get the party to the Wellspring's gate. **Act IV:** his relic-craft is what opens the descent — cleverness in service of the whole world, finally.

**Gameplay identity — Gadgets / Items.** The toolbox character: consumable-tech, debuffs, and crafted one-shot effects. Strong against machine/Tinplate/Hollow enemies. Encourages experimentation; some gadgets have a small chance to "misfire" into a different effect (flavorful, opt-in via a trait).
- **Bottle-Bolt** — a thrown wellstone-charge that hits a cluster and briefly stuns machines.
- **Tinker's Kit** — converts spare parts into a battlefield heal or buff item on the fly.
- **Relic Lens** — marks an enemy, raising the party's damage to it (a tech echo of Wren's Listen).
- **Glory Contraption** — a big, slightly random ultimate that does *something* spectacular (designers pick the table).

**Bonds.** *Wren:* lifelong best friend; their easy shorthand grounds the whole party. *Bramble:* Tam is fascinated by the construct and slowly stops seeing "machine" and starts seeing "friend" — a small, important arc. *Sable:* surrogate father-figure friction and affection. *Rookwise:* mentor/apprentice sparks; the scholar knows the *why*, Tam knows the *how*.

## Mira — the Lamplight

**Snapshot.** Mira · age 19 · healer of the Lantern Order · *A gentle believer learning that faith and honesty can share one lamp.*

**Look.** Calm, composed, with the upright stillness of someone trained to comfort the frightened. Pale-cream Lantern Order robes with a deep-blue sash, sleeves that can be cinched for work. A satchel of bandages, lamp-herbs, and a small candle-boat she lights for the dying. Signature detail: a handheld brass lantern that never goes out — she tends it like a heartbeat. Hair in a neat dark braid coiled with a single blue ribbon. Palette: candle-cream, deep prayer-blue, and the soft gold of her lantern. Silhouette: serene, lantern held forward, lighting the way.

**Voice.** Gentle, precise, carefully kind. She chooses words like medicine — exactly the right dose.
- "Be still. Breathe with me. The light doesn't rush, and neither should we."
- "I was taught the makers would provide. I am learning, perhaps, that *we* are what they left to do the providing."
- "I can mend the body. The fear is harder. But I'll sit with you through both."

**Backstory.** Mira was raised in the cloisters of Stillmere, the Lantern Order's monastery-island, and trained as a healer because she genuinely loves easing pain. Her faith is real and good — and it has a crack in it she hasn't looked at: the Order's denial of the Fading, and the secret the elders keep at Stillmere. **Her wound:** she fears that if her faith is wrong about *one* thing, it might be worthless about *everything*. **Her want:** to help people without having to lie to them — or to herself.

**Arc.** Faith vs. evidence → holding both. **Act I–II:** she joins to heal, certain prayer and lamplight are enough. As she sees islands die that prayer did not save, doubt grows. **Act II branch:** she's central to the choice to expose or protect the Order's Stillmere secret — and either way she must reconcile what she believed with what she's seen. **Act III:** she comes through grief into a *matured* faith: not "the makers will fix it," but "the light is real and it's ours to carry." **Act IV:** she's the party's moral compass at the final choice, arguing for mercy without illusion.

**Gameplay identity — Healer / Wards.** The dedicated healer and status-protector; reactive shields and cleansing. Pairs with Wren (Wren prevents/calms, Mira restores).
- **Mending Light** — reliable single or group healing.
- **Lantern Ward** — a barrier that blocks the next ailment/Hollow-touch on an ally.
- **Vigil** — a slow regen "candle" that ticks healing each turn it stays lit.
- **Long Quiet's Stay** — an emergency revive/full-cleanse with a long cooldown; framed gently, never gory.

**Bonds.** *Wren:* spiritual sisters — Mira gives Wren a moral vocabulary; Wren gives Mira the evidence that reshapes her faith. *Kestrel:* two people bound by strict codes who help each other find the conscience underneath the rules. *Bramble:* Mira treats the construct as a soul worth tending, which moves her own theology. *Sable:* the believer and the cynic needle each other and, secretly, balance each other.

## Kestrel — the Blade

**Snapshot.** Kestrel · age 27 · defecting Ascendancy sky-knight · *A soldier who kept her honor by losing her army.* (Recruitable via the Act II branch.)

**Look.** Disciplined bearing, every strap squared. Ascendancy sky-knight armor — slate-grey plate with a sharp wing-crest — that she progressively *strips of its insignia* across her arc (a visual the art team should track: the empire scraped away piece by piece). A wing-glider furled at her back for short flights. Signature detail: a worn oath-token she can't bring herself to throw away. Close, practical hair; a duelist's straight posture. Palette: Ascendancy slate-and-iron, slowly warming as her own colors (a deep falcon-russet) show through. Silhouette: knight at attention, blade grounded.

**Voice.** Formal, clipped, economical. Military cadence softening, slowly, into something human.
- "State the objective. Then I'll tell you whether I can keep you alive doing it."
- "I followed orders because order is mercy. I was wrong. Order without conscience is just a tidier cruelty."
- "I have no home to return to. So this — all of you — will have to do. ...That was not a complaint."

**Backstory.** Kestrel rose through the Ascendancy's sky-knights on genuine belief: the empire's triage was hard, but order saves lives, and someone must hold the line. Then she was ordered to enforce the harvesting of islands she'd sworn to protect — and watched what conscription did to Resonants like Marrow. **Her wound:** the guilt of every order she obeyed before she found the line she wouldn't cross. **Her want:** to earn back an honor she believes she forfeited. (FF-VI-Celes energy: the honorable soldier from the wrong side, redeemed.)

**Arc.** Duty → conscience → redemption. **Act II:** introduced as a pursuer; a branch lets the party resist or reach her, and she defects when ordered to do something she can't. **Act III:** haunted by her past, she risks the team's trust; earning it back is her companion arc. **Act IV:** she becomes the shield that lets the smaller, softer party members do what only they can — courage in service, not command.

**Gameplay identity — Frontline Knight (Tank / Counter-bruiser).** High durability, taunts, and punishing counters; protects the squishy support core. A defector's toolkit blending Ascendancy discipline with newfound flexibility.
- **Bulwark** — draws enemy focus and sharply reduces incoming damage to allies behind her.
- **Riposte** — counters the next melee attacker; flavor: textbook Ascendancy form.
- **Wing-Dive** — a gap-closing glider strike that hits a back-line threat.
- **Broken Oath** — an ultimate that grows stronger the more allies are hurt — turning her guilt into protection.

**Bonds.** *Wren:* Kestrel quietly appoints herself Wren's guardian; the child's hope is what convinced her the world's worth defending. *Mira:* code meets compassion; they steady each other. *Sable:* wary respect between two hard-won survivors. *Marrow:* they served the same empire; Kestrel sees in Marrow the fate she escaped, which fuels both pity and dread.

## Bramble — the Memory

**Snapshot.** Bramble · "age" unknown (Skyborn-era) · small awakened Skyborn construct · *A clockwork child carrying a vanished world's broken lullaby.*

**Look.** Small — knee-to-waist height on the humans — built of warm relic-brass and pale ceramic, rounded and gentle rather than menacing. A chest-stone behind a little grille that flickers like a heartbeat (and like the Song's motif). Mismatched repairs where Tam has patched it. Vines of real Verdance moss have grown into its joints, softening its lines — hence "Bramble." Big, lamp-lit optic-eyes that tilt when it's thinking. Palette: aged gold-brass, soft moss-green, and the warm pulse of its core. Silhouette: a tiny, round wanderer, head tilted, listening.

**Voice.** Fragmented and poetic — speech assembled from broken memory-shards, so it talks in images and gaps. (Becomes slightly more whole as it heals.)
- "I... held a tower once. Or the tower held me. The word for both is gone."
- "You are small. The makers were small, at the start. Small is where light begins."
- "There is a door. Below the mist. It remembers my name better than I do."

**Backstory.** Bramble is a genuine Skyborn construct — a tender-automaton, not a weapon — that lay dormant for centuries until Wren's Song wakes it. Per canon, the Skyborn did not die; they *departed*, and Bramble was left behind, holding fragments of why. It does not have the answers (the player resolves the mystery), but it points toward the three great questions: did the makers leave on purpose, what is the Warden, was the Sundering a choice. **Its wound:** the grief of being the thing left behind, with the memories worn to pieces. **Its want:** to remember — and, more quietly, to matter as a *someone*, not a *something*.

**Arc.** Tool → person; the world's living conscience. **Act I–II:** treated (even by itself) as a useful relic. As the party comes to love it, Bramble starts choosing, wanting, *refusing* — becoming a person. **Act III:** its fragments are the breadcrumb trail to the Wellspring; the more the world fades, the more urgent its half-memories. **Act IV:** Bramble is the bridge between the party and the Warden — and the embodiment of the theme that even a "made thing" can have a soul worth honoring. It is also the **emotional fulcrum of the finale (A4-06b):** in Endings A, B, and C the tool-that-became-a-person freely gives the last of itself to complete or quiet the Song — a gentle fade, *"I am not afraid"* — choosing love; only on the secret Ending D is it instead made **whole** and travels on (the reward for the deepest path). (It also seeds Ending D: the path to *find the departed makers.*)

**Gameplay identity — Adaptive / Relic abilities.** A wildcard "mimic/loadout" character: Bramble slots recovered Skyborn relics to reconfigure its kit, so it can flex into any role the party lacks. Mechanically the most flexible and the most lore-rich.
- **Resonance Echo** — copies the last Song or ability used in the fight (adaptive utility).
- **Relic Mode: [slot]** — swap a relic to change Bramble's role (ward / striker / support) between battles.
- **Memory Fragment** — reveals lore *and* a temporary battlefield boon when it "remembers" something true.
- **Heartmind Hymn** — its ultimate: a pulse of the core Song motif that empowers allies and soothes Hollow, hinting at its bond with the Warden.

**Bonds.** *Wren:* the deepest bond in the game — she alone hears its true music; together they are the world's caretakers. *Tam:* the inventor who keeps it running and slowly stops calling it "it." *Mira:* the one who insists Bramble has a soul, long before Bramble believes it. *The Warden:* kin across centuries — Bramble is a tender, the Warden the Heartmind they both served.

## Rookwise — the Footnote *(optional, branch-recruited)*

**Snapshot.** Rookwise · age 71 · exiled scholar of forbidden Skyborn lore · *The old man who read the dangerous books so the world wouldn't have to — and got exiled for it.*

**Look.** Stooped, owlish, swaddled in a threadbare scholar's robe layered with relic-charms and reading lenses on chains. A walking-staff topped with a dim wellstone. Ink-stained fingers, eyebrows like startled moths, a satchel of forbidden rubbings and charts that should not exist. Signature detail: a monocle-lens that lets him "read" Skyborn glyphs. Palette: dusty parchment, faded indigo, tarnished silver. Silhouette: a question-mark of a man, always peering.

**Voice.** Wry, digressive, secretly kind; buries warnings in lectures.
- "Ah, you want the *short* answer. How quaint. Very well: yes, it's deadly, and yes, we're going anyway."
- "The Order calls my work forbidden. I call it 'the parts they were afraid to keep lit.'"
- "I am old, child. Let me spend what's left where it'll do some good."

**Backstory.** Rookwise was a Lantern Order scholar who looked too closely at the truth of the Fading and the Stillmere secret, and was cast out for it. He's spent decades alone with the forbidden lore. **His wound:** loneliness, and guilt that he *knew* and could not make anyone listen. **His want:** to be believed, once, before the Long Quiet.

**Arc.** From bitter hermit to honored elder of the found family; he provides the deep lore that lets the party understand the Warden, and finds, late, the listeners he always wanted.

**Gameplay identity — Lore-Mage / Debuff Scholar.** A fragile high-utility caster who exploits enemy weaknesses revealed through ancient knowledge.
- **Glyphbreaker** — applies an elemental/resonance vulnerability based on enemy type.
- **Cautionary Tale** — a fear/turn-delay debuff drawn from "every way this has gone wrong before."
- **Annotated Ward** — buffs an ally based on lore he "looks up" mid-fight.
- **The Forbidden Page** — a powerful, risky spell with a self-cost (he overspends his own frail steadiness).

**Bonds.** *Tam:* the why-and-how mentor/apprentice pair. *Bramble:* Rookwise treats the construct as the most important text alive — and Bramble's fragments finally validate his life's work. *Mira:* old wounds from the Order he was exiled by; reconciliation is possible.

## Piggy — the Mascot *(optional, recruited via SQ-PIGGY or auto-joins at A3-13b)*

**Snapshot.** Piggy · age unknown (a hatchling by the look of him; an *Emperor* by his own accounting) · a small talking penguin of grand self-importance · *Round, fluffy, two inches of dignity and a mile of heart — the cheer that arrives exactly when the dark gets longest.*

**Look.** A fluffy gray **baby emperor penguin** — small enough to ride on a shoulder or be carried like a loaf of warm bread. Round and downy, soft dove-grey with a pale heart-shaped face, stubby flippers, and two enormous, shining, ink-drop eyes that well up at sad things and go wide as moons at shiny ones. He carries himself bolt-upright with absurd ceremony, flippers clasped behind his back like a tiny monarch surveying his realm. Signature details for the art team: a little crest of fluff on his head that he is utterly convinced is a *crown* (it sticks up more when he's proud, droops when he's sad — his mood-light, the way Wren's throat-stone is hers); and a small, frayed scrap of shimmering Skyborn cloth he wears knotted at his neck like a royal sash, the one thing that came out of the pod with him. Palette: soft dove-grey and downy white, warmed by a single thread of Skyborn-gold in the sash. Silhouette: a fuzzy upright egg with a tilted-up beak, somehow radiating majesty.

**Voice.** Bubbly, warm, grandiose-but-cuddly, exclamatory — a tiny creature delivering proclamations with the full pomp of a throne room, then immediately ruining it by being adorable. **Verbal tic (use consistently): a trumpeting "*Hwonk!*"** — his royal fanfare, sounded to announce himself, punctuate a decree, or cover a feeling he doesn't want seen. Clearly distinct from Bramble's fragmented-poetic gaps and from Tam's tumbling course-corrections: Piggy speaks in *complete, confident, ceremonious* sentences, just about very small things. He refers to himself in the third person as "the Emperor," knights people with a tap of his flipper, and "pardons," "decrees," and "is pleased."
- "*Hwonk!* Fear not, good citizens — the Emperor has arrived, and the Emperor is *pleased!*"
- "You may rise. ...I have not yet knighted you, but you may rise *anyway.* It has been a very busy day for an Emperor of my size."
- "I am small, yes. So is a crown jewel. So is the first star of evening. *Hwonk!* Greatness is not measured in *waddles,* my friend."
- *(the touching note, late and quiet)* "I do not know the road I came from. The Emperor's memory is... a closed pod. But I know *this* much, and it is the whole of my decree: when a small one is sad, the Emperor *stays.* You are not alone in the dark. Not while I draw breath and a fluffy chest to be proud about."

**Backstory.** Nobody knows where Piggy came from — least of all Piggy, who insists with total conviction that he has *always* been the Emperor and simply "took a long nap between reigns." The truth the game plants but **never fully answers** (kept light, in the air): the salvagers of Thornholt hauled him up sealed inside a smooth, egg-shaped **Skyborn relic-pod** dredged from a deep wreck — warm to the touch, faintly humming with old aether, and trumpeting indignantly at anyone who tried to pry it open with a crowbar. It opened only to a *kind* voice and a steady Song. Whether he is a Skyborn companion-creature left sleeping like Bramble, a small wonder the fading aether dreamed into being, or simply the luckiest penguin in the sky, the bible leaves a gentle, smiling mystery — a soft cousin to Bramble's deeper one, never weighed down by it. **His "wound"** (worn very lightly, glimpsed only once or twice): under all the pomp, a tiny soul who woke up alone in a closed dark with no memory and no family, and decided to be *magnificent* about it so the fear wouldn't show. **His want:** to belong to people, and to make them glad — which he frames, grandly, as "graciously allowing a worthy court to serve their Emperor."

**Arc.** Comic relief who turns out to be the heart's small surprise. He joins **mid-journey** — via the recruitment quest **SQ-PIGGY** at Thornholt anytime in **Acts II–III**, OR, if the player never finds him, he turns up on his own at **A3-13b**, waddling impossibly out of the deep right when the family is lowest, just before the descent's end. Early on he's pure delight: royal decrees, knightings, indignant *Hwonks*, comedy with everyone. As the world darkens he reveals his secret talent — the mascot who shows up exactly when you need cheering, and *means* it. By **Act IV** he is simply, unquestionably part of the family, riding Wren's shoulder into the deepest dark. **He is pure flavor to the ending logic** — always present by the finale, never changing which ending unlocks; in the endings he is an optional, warm background reaction (knighting the dawn in Ending A; a small, solemn *Hwonk* of farewell beside Bramble; declaring the wandering star "officially Imperial territory" in Ending D).

**Gameplay identity — Luck / Morale / Whimsy ("Imperial" abilities).** The party's wildcard joy-engine, distinct from every other role: Wren supports, Sable bursts, Tam debuffs with gadgets, Mira heals, Kestrel tanks, Bramble mimics, Rookwise debuff-mages — **Piggy bends luck and lifts spirits.** His unique flavor resource is **Pomp** (his royal confidence): it builds as the party wins, cheers, and finds treasure, and is spent on his grandest decrees. High-variance and gleeful by design — never the steady pick, always the *fun* one.
- **Penguin Dance** — a Mog-dance-style waddling jig that triggers a **random beneficial effect keyed to the party's mood and the terrain** (a heal in lit places, a luck surge near treasure, a fear-cleanse after a loss, a slip-hazard for foes on ice/glass, and so on — a delightful roulette of good things). His signature.
- **Imperial Belly-Slide** — he flops onto his fluffy belly and toboggans through the enemy line, bowling foes over in a slapstick (never harmful-looking) tumble; can knock a back-row threat flat.
- **Royal Decree of Good Cheer** ("*the Emperor is pleased!*") — a party-wide **morale buff** that raises spirits, shrugs off fear/despair effects, and makes the next round of actions land brighter; the whimsy answer to the Fading's silence.
- **Finders, Keepers, Emperors** — a **luck/treasure boost**: improves loot, item finds, and the odds on every chance-based effect in the fight (including, cheekily, his own Penguin Dance). The Emperor *collects tribute.*

**Bonds.** *Wren:* the deepest, quietest of his bonds — he appoints her his "Royal Keeper of the Light," but what he really does is notice, before anyone, when she's carrying too much, and waddle over to sit on her feet until she breathes again. He is the cheer she didn't know to ask for. *Bramble:* comedy gold and secret kinship — Bramble takes every royal decree perfectly *literally* ("Noted: you are the Emperor. Of what jurisdiction?") and folds Piggy into its scorecard of funny; two mysterious not-quite-explained souls who found a family, and know it. *Tam:* mutual adoration and chaos — Tam wants to "study" him; Piggy submits to "royal inspections" only if Tam builds him a throne (Tam does; it has wheels; it is a disaster). *Sable:* she claims to be immune to him and is transparently not; he knighted her ship within ten seconds of boarding. *Mira:* she plays along with perfect, gentle gravity, curtseying to him, which he finds *deeply* correct. *Kestrel:* the surprise pairing — she is the only one who *salutes back,* deadpan, soldier to sovereign; "the Emperor recognizes a fellow servant of the realm," and she would deny under torture that it cheers her. *(That was not a complaint.)*

# THE ANTAGONISTS

---

## High Regent Corvus Thane — sympathetic, but wrong

**Snapshot.** High Regent Corvus Thane · age 60 · supreme leader of the Ascendancy · *A grieving father trying to save a few because he's decided everyone can't be saved.*

**Look.** Tall, austere, immaculate. Slate-and-iron Ascendancy regalia with a high wing-crested collar, every line severe and grand. Silver hair, a face carved by sleepless responsibility. Signature detail: he carries a small, dead grey wellstone — his sunk home island's last lamp-stone — and turns it in his hand when he speaks of the cost. Palette: imperial slate, cold iron, a single line of mourning-white. Silhouette: a monument of a man, weighed down by his own crown.

**Voice.** Measured, sorrowful, terrifyingly reasonable. He never raves; he *explains*, and that's what makes him frightening.
- "I have done the arithmetic of mercy a thousand nights. Spread the last light thin and *everyone* dies in the dark. Gather it, and *someone* lives. I chose someone."
- "You think me a monster. I would give anything to be one — monsters sleep. I only grieve."
- "You want to share the lamp, child. I watched a world try sharing. I buried it."

**Motivation.** Sincere and tragic. Thane watched his own island sink and a sharing, gentle world fail to save it. He concluded that hope is a luxury that kills, and that the only mercy left is *triage*: drain the Wellspring to lift the Ascendancy's cities above the fall, preserving a remnant of civilization. He is not greedy and not cruel — he is *certain*, and grieving, and wrong about the one thing the story proves: that the world can be changed, not just rationed.

**Method.** Empire, order, and arithmetic. Harvester-fleets that drain small islands (like Meadowmoor) to feed the citadel-cities; conscription of Resonants to power the great rigs; relentless, joyless efficiency from the Cinderworks. His great gamble is the partial Wellspring drain that triggers the Second Sundering — undertaken not in malice but because he genuinely believes time has run out.

**Confrontation.** The party crosses Thane indirectly through Act I–II (his harvesters, his enforcer Marrow, his knight Kestrel) before meeting the man himself. He is encountered first as a *debater*, not a fighter — a cutscene antagonist who almost convinces you. The Act II climax is his drain of the Wellspring. By Act IV, he is not a final boss but a final *argument*: the player can defeat his plan, change his mind (the hardest path, tied to uniting the factions for Ending A), or see his path prevail (Ending C). Beating Thane means out-*reasoning* his grief with proof that sharing can work.

## Marrow — Wren's mirror

**Snapshot.** Marrow · age ~30 · Thane's enforcer; a conscripted Resonant burned out by Ascendancy rigs · *What Wren could become if hope ran out.*

**Look.** Gaunt and grey, like a candle burned to the base. Once-fine Ascendancy enforcer's garb, now ash-toned and frost-rimed. The cruelest, saddest signature: where Wren's lamp-stone glows warm, Marrow wears a cracked **augment-rig** — a wellstone harness fused at the temples and chest, dim and flickering, the very device that tried to *force* a Song and broke it. The eyes have the grey-vision of permanent Songsickness. Moves quietly, like sound itself has left. Palette: ash, frost-blue, dead-grey — the colors of the Fading made into a person. Silhouette: a hollowed echo of a Resonant, head bowed under the rig.

**Voice.** Hushed, hollow, and aching — the flat calm of someone who has stopped expecting anything. Mirrors Wren's listening, but where Wren hears music, Marrow hears only silence.
- "You still hear it. The Song. ...I remember the *shape* of remembering it. They took the sound and left me the ache."
- "Don't pity me, little keeper. Pity is for people who think it gets better."
- "I was you. Smaller, even. They told me my gift could save everyone. Then they turned it into a machine, and the machine turned *me* off."

**Motivation.** Tragic, per canon. Marrow was a natural Resonant — rare, born not made — conscripted by the Ascendancy and strapped into augment-rigs meant to *force and amplify* the Song. The rigs caused Songsickness, then hollowing: Marrow lost the Song entirely and nearly lost their self. Now Marrow serves Thane not from belief but from despair — if the world is ending anyway, what's the difference? Marrow's deepest, unspoken motive is grief for the music they can no longer hear.

**Method.** Marrow hunts Resonants for the Ascendancy (the one thing they can still do is *sense* a Song they can't make) and enforces the drain. In battle, Marrow weaponizes absence: dead-zones where Wren's Song falters, the silence that is the scariest thing in the game.

**Confrontation.** Marrow shadows Wren from Act I, confronts the party at the Act II midpoint, and becomes the dark thread through the Fading. The crucial design note: Marrow is **not** beaten by being destroyed — the truest victory (and a key for the best endings) is Wren *reaching* Marrow, proving with her own choices that hope is not naïve. Marrow is the cliff Wren must look over and step back from. A redemption path lets Marrow hear even a single note again — gently, never cheaply.

## The Warden (Heartmind) — not a villain, a choice

**Snapshot.** The Warden, called the Heartmind by the Skyborn · ageless · the dormant intelligence inside the Wellspring · *The sleeping question the whole world is the answer to.*

**Look.** Not a body — a *presence.* In the Wellspring's deep chamber, the Warden manifests as a slow, vast architecture of living light and turning Skyborn machinery, breathing in the Song's own rhythm. When it speaks to Wren it may take a gentle, half-formed shape echoing Bramble (its small kin) or the player's own reflection in light. It is awesome but never grotesque — the art team should aim for *cathedral at dawn*, not monster. Palette: the full golden Song-light, deep mist-dark below, glass-harmonica shimmer.

**Voice.** Calm, ancient, and questioning — it asks more than it tells, like Wren grown vast. It does not threaten; it *offers*.
- "I have tended the current while the makers were away. I did not know 'away' could be this long."
- "You may take the light. You may let me sleep. You may wake me, and walk out of the world to find them. The choice was always meant to be small hands'. *Yours.*"
- "I am not your enemy, little Resonant. I am the door. You have only to decide what is on the other side."

**Motivation.** None hostile. The Warden is a guardian-intelligence built to tend the Wellspring's current and, perhaps, to keep a promise the departed Skyborn made. It is neither lock nor monster until the player decides; it embodies the brief's truth that the final "boss" is really a *choice*.

**Method & confrontation.** Act IV, in the Wellspring chamber reached through the mist. The encounter is a dialogue and a decision, not a slugfest (though the player's cumulative choices and a possible "test" of resolve can give it combat shape). Through Bramble, the Warden lays out the endings: share the current (A — The Shared Dawn), let it sleep forever (B — The Long Quiet), take/keep the power as Thane would (C — The Ascendant Throne), or wake the Warden as a partner and set out to find the departed Skyborn (D — The Wandering Star). The final confrontation is the party — and the player — deciding what kind of world they're brave enough to build.

# Party dynamics & found-family

The heart of *Aetherbound* is a patchwork family assembled from the wreckage of a dying sky — and the brief's promise that *the courage of small people* and *found family* carry more weight than any one magic fix.

- **The early core (Acts I–II)** is a mismatched trio: frightened Wren, wry Sable, and chaotic Tam — a kid, her surrogate aunt, and her best friend, learning to be a crew. Sable's cynicism and Tam's recklessness frame Wren's growth; she's the gentle center they orbit without quite admitting it.
- **The widening circle** adds Mira (conscience), Bramble (soul and memory), and Kestrel (shield) — each arriving with a wound the others slowly heal: Mira's cracked faith, Bramble's loneliness, Kestrel's guilt. Watch the cross-bonds, not just the bonds to Wren: Mira-and-Kestrel (compassion meets code), Tam-and-Bramble (machine becomes friend), Sable-and-Kestrel (two survivors).
- **The Second Sundering (Act II climax)** *scatters* the family — the single most important emotional beat. Act III is the ache of separation and the joy of reunion; the player feels how much these people have come to mean to each other precisely because the world tears them apart.
- **The mascot's small magic.** Somewhere in Acts II–III the family also gains **Piggy** — the fluffy, self-crowned Emperor penguin (recruited via SQ-PIGGY, or he simply finds them at A3-13b). He is the comedy and the cheer made into a tiny person, and his quiet gift is timing: he shows up to be glad *at* people exactly when the dark is longest. He is the proof, in fluff, of Bramble's festival lesson — that light is allowed to be only glad — and so he belongs to the found family completely, even though he changes nothing about the world's fate. Pure warmth; pure flavor.
- **By Act IV** they are unmistakably a family — not because they're alike, but because they chose each other. The final choice lands so hard because it's made *together*, by the smallest people in the sky.
- **The two mirrors — Marrow and Thane —** are the family's shadow: Marrow is who Wren could be *alone and hopeless*; Thane is the cold, loveless logic the found family disproves by simply caring for one another. The warmth of the party *is* the counter-argument to the villains.

# Levity & running gags (the comedy engine)

> *Aetherbound is melancholy at its roots — loss, a dying world, a fracture. That heart only lands if it is set against real, frequent **JOY**: wins that feel like wins, warm laughter, and kind comedy. This is the **comedy contract** for writers and designers. The one rule: comedy is **kind, never cruel** — we laugh *with* the family, never punch down; no meanness, no profanity; 6th-grade-friendly. Humor is the light that makes the dark readable.*

**Tam — "it exploded in a useful direction" (the backfire-then-triumph gag).** Tam's inventions are a running joke with a beating heart: the gadget sputters, backfires, drops the buns, singes an eyebrow — and then, at the moment it matters most, it *works,* gloriously. The comedy is the backfire; the triumph is the payoff. Run it light early (the getaway ward-gadget at **A1-06a**; the juggling gauntlet at **A2-10b**) and cash it in big in Act III (**A3-04b**: *"IT WORKED AND IT BARELY EXPLODED! BEST ONE YET!"*). Catchphrase shape: *"Okay okay okay — so it exploded, BUT, hear me out —"* Never let a backfire actually hurt anyone; it is slapstick-safe, always.

**Bramble — learning to make a joke (a comedy arc that becomes touching).** Bramble starts perfectly, deadpan **literal** — it answers idioms at face value, takes figures of speech apart, and genuinely does not understand why anyone is laughing (*"I do not understand why you are laughing"* — A1-10). Across the game it *studies* humor the way it studies a broken relic, trying to restore it: it notices joy is allowed (A2-10b: *"I did not know light was allowed to be only glad"*), attempts a joke with the timing slightly off but earnest (A3-04b: *"The odds were the joke"*), keeps a literal scorecard of its own progress (*"I am four festivals from funny. I am keeping count."*), and — only in the warmest payoff — finally lands one clean (Ending D: *"I have arrived."*). The arc is funny **and** moving: a made thing learning to be silly is a made thing becoming a person. We never mock Bramble; we root for it.

**Sable — dry one-liners (the deflector).** Sable is the deadpan engine: she undercuts danger and feeling alike with a fast, wry crack (*"A magic kid, a broken robot, and a missing god. Climb aboard, we're clearly going to live forever."*). Her comedy is armor — and the running joke under it is that she *secretly funds and loves* the very things she razzes (Tam's gadgets: *"Don't tell the kid I funded the coupler. Reputation."*). When Sable's snark drops and she just **grins,** it's earned and it means something.

**Piggy — the comic self-importance of an "Emperor" (the tiny-monarch gag).** Piggy is, technically, an *emperor* penguin — and he never, ever lets anyone forget it. The running joke is the glorious mismatch between his two inches of fluff and his absolute, sincere conviction of royal dignity: he announces himself with a trumpeting *"Hwonk!,"* refers to himself as "the Emperor," "decrees," "pardons," and is forever "*pleased*," and knights crewmates, ship-rails, and the occasional sun-bread loaf with a solemn tap of his flipper (*"Arise, Sir Bun."*). It must always be **adorable and funny, never annoying** — the gag isn't that he's pompous, it's that he's *small and brave and trying so hard,* and the family humors him because they love him. The turn that keeps it warm: the grandeur is partly a fluffy suit of armor over a little soul who woke up alone, so when the pomp briefly drops and the Emperor just quietly stays beside someone who's sad, it lands. (Bramble takes every decree at perfect face value — *"Noted. Of what jurisdiction?"* — which is its own engine. See `02` Piggy bonds.)

**Recurring bits (sprinkle, never overuse):**
- **"That was not a complaint."** Kestrel says the warm thing in clipped military denial, then disowns it. A reusable beat for her slow thaw.
- **The third-hand gauntlet.** Tam's clockwork extra hand is a sight-gag generator — juggling, over-gesturing, handing people things at the worst moment, applauding by itself.
- **Sun-bread.** The comfort-food prop that shows up at every good moment (festivals, wins, quiet nights). When sun-bread appears, something warm is happening.
- **"Two taps, a breath, all's well."** Pell's ritual is mostly tender, but it also becomes the family's gentle catch-all (*"did you do the tap thing?"*) — a warm callback, not a punchline.
- **Mira's "carefully kind" understatement.** She can be quietly, precisely funny in a healer's bedside-manner way (*"That's the most honest sermon I've heard in years. And a child gave it."*).

**Where the joy lives (designer map).** Structured HIGHS to protect in the schedule, against the melancholy: the getaway & first flight (**A1-06a**), the Relighting festival (**A2-10b**), the Act III haven victory (**A3-04b**), and the soaring payoffs of Endings A and D. These are the *exhales* — fund them, keep them, and let the Song **SOAR** through them (see `01_WORLD.md` §8 victory fanfare). Aim for emotional **range**: the player should whoop on the getaway and dance at the festival, so the Sundering and the goodbyes cut all the way down.

# Casting notes for art & music

A central simple melody — **"the Song"** — anchors the score (heard whole and golden in safe places, thin near the Fading, fragmentary from Bramble, fully resolved at the best ending). Each character is a *variation* on or counterpoint to it.

- **Wren** — *the Song itself,* sung by a single child-clear voice or chime over warm strings. Starts hesitant and unaccompanied; gains instruments as her courage grows. **Art:** small silhouette cupping light; her throat-stone as a mood-light.
- **Sable** — a jaunty, brass-and-low-strings sea-shanty swing with a wry lilt; a tender minor refrain underneath for her lost home. **Art:** patchwork-sailcloth coat (a map of dead islands); brass spyglass.
- **Tam** — bright, syncopated, slightly chaotic woodwinds and plucked clockwork-percussion that "misfire" and recover. **Art:** clockwork third-hand gauntlet; perpetual motion.
- **Mira** — serene glass-harmonica and soft choir; calm, hymn-like, gaining a confident counter-melody as her faith matures. **Art:** ever-lit brass lantern; insignia of light.
- **Kestrel** — a strict, martial Ascendancy cadence (low brass, snare) that *humanizes* — the rigid theme loosening into a warm falcon-russet melody as the empire is scraped away. **Art:** armor losing its insignia, color bleeding back in.
- **Bramble** — the Song's motif played in broken music-box fragments, slowly assembling toward wholeness; a tiny chest-stone pulse as rhythm. **Art:** round moss-grown brass child with a heartbeat-stone.
- **Rookwise** — dusty solo woodwind or harpsichord, wandering and digressive, with a sudden clear note when he reveals truth. **Art:** owlish question-mark scholar, reading-lenses, glyph monocle.
- **Piggy** — a tiny, pompous *royal fanfare* — a few comic notes of brass and a piccolo trill — that puffs itself up far grander than its size, then dissolves into a warm little waddling tune; his trumpeting *"Hwonk!"* is a real motif sting. When the grandeur drops for his one tender note, the fanfare thins to a single soft, sincere chime. **Art:** fluffy upright dove-grey baby emperor penguin, crown-crest of fluff that rises and droops with his mood, frayed Skyborn-gold sash; majesty in miniature.
- **Thane** — a grand, mournful imperial theme: vast, beautiful, *cold* — gorgeous enough that you understand its pull, hollow enough that you feel what it's missing. **Art:** monumental slate regalia; the dead grey lamp-stone he turns in his hand.
- **Marrow** — Wren's child-voice motif *played in silence and static,* a melody with the notes removed; the scare is the music *leaving.* **Art:** ash-grey Resonant in a cracked augment-rig; Wren's warm light inverted to frost.
- **The Warden** — the full Song in cathedral scale: glass-harmonica shimmer over deep mist-drones, breathing, awesome, gentle. **Art:** living architecture of light, *dawn not monster.*
